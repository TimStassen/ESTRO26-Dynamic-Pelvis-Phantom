ESTRO26: Dynamic Tissue-Equivalent Pelvis Phantom

Tim Stassen, Luana de Freitas Nascimento, Frank Verhaegen and Gabriel Paiva Fonseca

Main Objective

Modern online adaptive radiotherapy (oART) adjusts treatment in real time to account for anatomical changes. However, verifying dose delivery remains challenging, especially when organs change rapidly, such as bladder filling.

  • Traditional phantoms lack realistic anatomy
  • Limited ability to simulate organ motion

This can lead to less reliable validation of treatment workflows.

Our solution

We developed a realistic 3D-printed pelvic phantom that includes:

  • Detailed anatomical structures (muscle, fat, bone)
  • A dynamic bladder with controlled filling and emptying

Developing the anthrophic phantom

The developed tissue-equivalent anthropomorphic phantom, was developed using the following steps:

The first step is to select a wide variety of "candidate" materials. From these materials, samples are printed and scanned on a Dual Energy CT in step 2 to determine their material properties: the relative electron density (RED) and effective atomic number (Zeff). In step 3, different settings were tested to vary the RED value of the printed region using a specific filament. An optimal RED value is determined for each combination of printer settings and the closest match with a reference value (here the ICRU44 by woodward et al). once the optimal setting for all relevant anatomical structures were obtained, they are used with the anatomical geometries for phantom preparation. The phantom is then printed and scanned on the DECT to verify the tissue properties within the phantom.

Bladder development

The phantom (187 × 287 × 70 mm³) was created using two 3D-printing materials and includes nine anatomical structures. Printing parameters were optimized to match realistic CT values using in-house software (AMIGOpy).

Dynamic bladder system:
  • Hollow bladder cavity with two channels
  • Balloon connected to a peristaltic pump
  • Water-equivalent gel mimics surrounding tissue

Measurements

The Bladder phantom was scanned using a:

  • CT (120 kV)
  • CBCT (125 kV)

and Bladder filling volumes:

  • 10 ml
  • 40 ml
  • 70 ml

A saline-water mixture was used to improve image contrast. Volumes were segmented using 3D Slicer and compared to actual filling volumes.

Results

Hounsfield Units (realism):

Tissue CT (HU) CBCT (HU)
Skeletal muscle 157 161
Adipose tissue 24.5 19.0
Cortical bone 596 761

Bladder volume accuracy:

  • 10 ml: deviation ~10–13%
  • 40 ml: deviation ~4–5%
  • 70 ml: deviation ~1–2%

Absolute differences remained small (0.9–1.9 ml).

Conclusion

This study demonstrates a realistic phantom with a controllable dynamic bladder, enabling more accurate simulation of anatomical changes during treatment.

  • Improved verification of oART workflows
  • More realistic testing conditions
  • Potential for complex clinical scenario simulation

Looking ahead

Further development of dynamic phantoms could improve clinical realism and enhance adaptive radiotherapy quality.
